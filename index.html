Our Heritage

A Family Tradition
Since Safi, Morocco

Our father, Roger Madar, owned Comosa — a sardine fishing and canning company on the Atlantic coast of Morocco in the 1950s. When the family moved to Montreal in the 1960s, he brought the craft with him, sourcing fresh Grey Mullet and curing bottarga by hand every week.


For decades, bottarga was a Friday night ritual — sliced thin, served with Arak before dinner. Brothers Herbert and Jean grew up with that taste. Now they bring it to you.

What Is
Bottarga?

Bottarga is made from the roe sacs of wild-caught Grey Mullet (Mugil Cephalus). The sacs are cleaned, enrobed in coarse salt, then naturally dried until they reach a firm, cured state — like a fine hard cheese, but from the sea.

Just two ingredients: fish roe and salt. No additives. No preservatives. A 3,000-year-old recipe, unchanged.

How to Enjoy

Like a Fine Cheese,
but From the Sea

Serve bottarga at room temperature for the fullest flavor. Use a sharp, non-serrated knife to slice it thin — like a fine charcuterie. Peel the wax from waxed varieties only after it reaches room temperature.

Re-wrap any open piece tightly in plastic, removing all air. Always store refrigerated.

Shave or grate it over pasta, rice, eggs, or toast. Savor slices as an aperitif. The more you explore, the more you'll find.
