What Is Bottarga?

The ancient cured roe of wild-caught Grey Mullet. Two ingredients — fish roe and salt — and nearly three millennia of craft. This is what taste buds dream of.

The Basics

A Centuries-Old
Briny Delicacy

Bottarga is made from the roe or eggs of wild-caught Grey Mullet (Mugil Cephalus). Each fish contains two lobes of roe, each cradled in its own natural sac.

The sacs are carefully removed whole, thoroughly cleaned, then enrobed in coarse salt. They are then naturally dried or dehydrated until they reach a firm, cured state — somewhere between a hard sausage and an aged cheese in texture.

Once cured, they are immediately preserved — either in shrink wrap, or dipped multiple times in warm melted paraffin wax, an old-school method that has been used for centuries before modern preservation existed.

The longer the cure, the sharper the flavor and the darker the color. Some like it mild; some like it sharp. We carry both.

Origins

Nearly 3,000 Years
of History

The Phoenicians — 1550 BC

Bottarga dates back nearly 3,000 years to the Phoenicians, whose enterprising maritime trading culture spread across the Mediterranean from 1550 BC to 300 BC. They are believed to be the first culture to salt and dry Grey Mullet Roe.

Ancient Egypt

Drawings of fishermen preparing Mullet roe have been discovered along the banks of the Nile on ancient Egyptian murals. Mediterranean peoples remain today's biggest Bottarga connoisseurs and consumers.

Written Into Literature — 1465

Cured Mullet Roe was first written into literature by Bartolomeo Sacchi (Platina), an Italian writer and gastronomist who wrote the first ever printed cookbook "De Honesta Voluptate et Valetudine" ("On Honourable Pleasure and Health"), ca. 1465.

A Truly Global Delicacy

Italians can be credited with best popularizing the term "Bottarga" — short for Bottarga di Muggine (Roe of Mullet) — the most universal term for cured mullet roe today.

Today, Bottarga is widely enjoyed throughout Europe — most popular in Italy, France, Spain, Greece, and Egypt. Japan, Taiwan, Portugal, Croatia, North Africa, Lebanon and Turkey each have their own name and preparation methods for virtually the same ancient delicacy.

Used by chefs including Martha Stewart, Frankie Celenza, and Jamie Oliver, and featured on CNN's Anthony Bourdain: Parts Unknown (Marseille and Charleston, 2015).

How to Enjoy

Serving & Care

Bottarga is best enjoyed at room temperature, like a fine cheese. Here's everything you need to know.

🌡️

Serve at Room Temperature

Always bring bottarga to room temperature before serving. Cold bottarga mutes the flavor. Let it rest 15–20 minutes out of the refrigerator.

🔪

Use a Sharp, Non-Serrated Knife

A super sharp, non-serrated knife gives you a clean cut without shredding the roe. For waxed varieties, let them reach room temperature first — the wax peels easily.

🎁

Peel Waxed Varieties Carefully

For Boutargue Classique and other waxed lobes, peel the paraffin wax off carefully once at room temperature. The wax is purely for preservation — the roe inside is what you're after.

🧊

Store Refrigerated

Always keep bottarga refrigerated. For best results, re-wrap any open piece tightly in plastic film, removing all air pockets. Shrink-wrap if possible.

🍝

Grate Like a Truffle

Grate bottarga over pasta, rice, eggs, or toast using a fine grater or microplane. It transforms an ordinary dish into something extraordinary.

🥂

Aperitif Slices

Cut into thin slices and serve as an aperitif — with Arak, a dry white, or prosecco. This is how it's been enjoyed for centuries across the Mediterranean.
